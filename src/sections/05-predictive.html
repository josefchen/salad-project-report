Section 05

Predicting Who Won't Come Back

Which customers are at risk of leaving — and what can we do about it?

What this does

After a customer places their 2nd order, the model looks at everything about their visits so far — how much they spent, how long between visits, what they ordered, which channel they used — and gives them a risk score: how likely are they to never come back?

A customer is labelled “churned” if they placed zero further orders before the end of the data — but only when their silence is long enough to be meaningful (at least 3× their typical gap between visits). Customers who might just be between orders are excluded, so every label is high-confidence. The model ranks customers from highest to lowest risk. The table below shows: if you focus on the riskiest 20%, how many actual leavers do you find?

Delivery platform customers (Deliveroo, UberEats) are excluded — we can’t identify them across visits. All numbers are from a test set of customers the model has never seen before.

Key Insight

After just 2 visits, the model can identify customers likely to disappear. Focusing on the top 5% highest-risk customers finds 1.9× more churners than picking at random — that's where focused, higher-cost actions pay off. The top 20% still delivers 1.5× lift with lighter interventions.

Key Insight

Each retained customer is worth ~£70 in subsequent orders. With 4,679 regulars currently at risk, that’s up to ~£327K in revenue that could quietly disappear — or be saved with timely intervention.

Key Insight

Most customers (82%) are till-only — we can score their risk but cannot email or call them. Any retention action for these customers has to happen in-store, when they're physically present. The 18% who use digital channels (Click & Collect, Kiosk) can be contacted directly.

What to do with the scores
  • Top 5% risk: small group, high confidence — worth focused attention. Personal follow-up for digital-channel customers; extra care at the counter for till-only customers.
  • Top 20% risk: larger group — suits lighter actions. Automated messages for digital-channel customers; loyalty sign-up prompts at the till for till-only customers.
  • Compare churn rates by store to spot locations that lose customers faster than expected — a high churn store may have an experience problem, not a marketing problem.
